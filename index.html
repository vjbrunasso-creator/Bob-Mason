GRANDPA BOB MASON 1933 — 202?

A Life Worth Living

Grandpa Bob Mason was born on Friday, January 13, 1933, in the rural community of Solon, Ohio, to William (Wm) and Elmira Mason. His life was shaped early by the influence of his barnstorming aviator father and a determined mother who made it clear that her two sons, Bob and Jim, were going to succeed. They did.

Bob retired as an Executive Water and Power Resource Planner with the Salt River Project (SRP). His brother, Jim, retired as a Lieutenant with the Maricopa County Sheriff’s Department and later served as Mayor of Wickenburg, Arizona.

The brothers shared a lifelong commitment to leadership, particularly through their mutual passion for off‑highway vehicle (OHV) activities. Jim led the High Mountain Search and Rescue Organization, while Bob served as President then later as Advisor to the Board of Directors of the American Sand Association, a national OHV advocacy organization representing the sand‑duning community. Bob also conceived and co-founded ICSAC.

In 1947, the Mason family relocated to Morristown, Arizona (Castle Hot Springs Junction), about 15 miles east of Wickenburg. They purchased 80 acres along the Hassayampa River, three miles south of town. There, they drilled a well, built a house and shop, and graded a runway to support Bob’s father’s livelihood and passion for aviation.When Bob began the 10th grade at Wickenburg High School in 1948, he met his first life mentor, Bob Olson, an English teacher and amateur (ham) radio enthusiast. Olson recognized Bob’s interest in electronics and launched a high school radio class by acquiring surplus World War II radio equipment. Bob’s father had also been a longtime ham radio operator in Ohio, further nurturing his curiosity.

Bob’s second life mentors were Ethel and Perry Underdown, founders of a startup lumber company in Wickenburg. They hired Bob as a high‑school student to handle odd jobs, a relationship that lasted eight years: three years during high school, four years while Bob attended Arizona State University, and one year afterward while he searched for work in his chosen field.

The Underdowns gave Bob a great deal of responsibility, providing invaluable hands‑on experience. He did everything imaginable: bookkeeping, constructing an apartment at the lumberyard, delivering orders, hauling lumber from the sawmill, and repairing virtually anything mechanical. These years honed his practical skills and work ethic.

Bob’s third life mentor was Charlie Merritt, an ASU professor, Chief Engineer for Phoenix PBS Channel 8, and former Chief Engineer for the Voice of America during World War II. Like Bob Olson, Merritt established new programs by securing surplus WWII electronics equipment, this time to support Channel 8 and a newly created ASU electronics curriculum. Bob graduated in 1954 with a Bachelor of Science in Electronics.

Before meeting his fourth and most influential mentor, Ted Hardy, Bob found his way to SRP through a fellow ASU alumnus, Curtis Arnett, who turned down a job offer and recommended Bob instead.

Bob was hired by Ted Hardy as a Junior Engineer on Friday, June 13, 1955. He worked under Hardy until 1974, when Hardy was promoted to Manager of Power Resource Development. Bob was chosen to replace him as Supervisor of Operational Engineering in the Power Operations Department.

When Ted Hardy passed away in 1976, Jack Pfister, SRP’s General Manager, selected Bob to succeed Hardy as Manager of Power Resource Development. By that time, Bob had spent 21 years in Power Operations.

During their years together, it became clear that Ted Hardy was more than a mentor; he was Bob’s advocate. With Hardy’s guidance and Pfister’s encouragement, Bob became a trusted staff member for special assignments and extra‑curricular responsibilities, both mission‑related and community‑focused.

Bob eagerly embraced these roles, which showcased his leadership and management abilities. His service included President of the PERA Employee Recreation Club, President of the SRP Employee Booster Association supporting the Phoenix United Fund, and 20 years as an SRP apprentice instructor.

In 1968, as SRP’s representative to the Phoenix Junior Chamber of Commerce, Bob served as Chairman of the Unlimited Hydroplane Races at Lake Pleasant, a role that required a 30‑day furlough from his regular SRP duties.

During Bob’s career, SRP experienced major growth and transformation. Under Hardy’s leadership, Bob played a key role in evaluating new power resources and negotiating contracts. Projects under consideration included joint‑venture coal‑fired power plants, extra‑high‑voltage transmission systems, pump storage, conversion of SRP hydroelectric plants from 25‑cycle to 60‑cycle power, and development of the Palo Verde Nuclear Generating Station.

The 1955 Power Coordination Agreement with APS opened the door to power pooling, further expanding SRP’s influence. As Hardy’s senior engineer, Bob’s responsibilities were extensive.

Later, Jack Pfister transferred Bob to the Water Group, where he worked for Reid Teeples as Director of River Studies. Bob’s duties included dam safety design, cost‑sharing negotiations, and developing planning processes to guide SRP’s transition from agricultural water delivery to urban consumers.

This role brought Bob into frequent direct collaboration with Pfister. As GM staff meeting coordinator, Bob worked closely with Reid and Pfister on critical water‑resource initiatives.

During his final decade at SRP, Bob was actively involved with the National Water Resources Association and the Colorado River Water Users Association, including planning and implementing national conventions in Phoenix.

Grandpa Bob was a self‑taught craftsman who could build or fix nearly anything. He retired for more than 30 years, during which he built several rat rods and traveled extensively with his wife, Sharon.

Even while managing a demanding career, Bob remained deeply committed to his role as a father, grandfather, and family patriarch. As active outdoor enthusiasts, family weekends were often spent at the lake, in the mountains, or exploring desert trails. His support and presence were constant priorities.

Before and after retirement, Bob and Sharon enjoyed motorhome adventures and worldwide cruises together, fully embracing the life they built side by side.
